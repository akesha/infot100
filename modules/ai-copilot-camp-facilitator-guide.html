PAGE 2 · WHY & WHEN

§ 01Why this module exists

Most students arrive with one of two unhelpful relationships to AI: dependency ("I can't write a single line without it") or refusal ("Real CS students don't use AI"). Neither serves them. The job market they're entering rewards a third posture — critical, fluent co-pilot use — and almost no class teaches it explicitly.

This module names that third posture and gives students reps. By the end of 90 minutes they have written, scored, and been audited on a real prompt for one of their actual assignments, and they leave with a one-page journal template they'll run for five days.

THE ANCHOR CONCEPT The Co-pilot Loop: FRAME → PROBE → VERIFY → OWN. When OWN sends you back to FRAME with a smaller, sharper question — that's the work. Everything in the module ladders up to this loop.

§ 02Outcomes

By the end of class, students will be able to:

  • Identify confidently-wrong AI responses on a topic they know.
  • Articulate the four moves of the Co-pilot Loop in their own words.
  • Engineer a prompt at three escalating fidelities (Vague → FRAME → full Loop) for a real assignment.
  • Audit a peer's prompt across four axes — specific, scoped, verifiable, ethical — with a written verdict.
  • Track their AI use honestly enough to spot a personal pattern across five days.

§ 05
00 · Trust Fall
10 MIN · 00:00 — 00:10 · SLIDES 02–03
PHASE 1 0:00 — 0:03  ·  SETUP

Don't tell anyone next to you what you picked yet. You're going to see a Python question and three AI answers. Two are confidently wrong. One is right. Pick the one you'd trust.

  • Show slide 02. Read the question aloud.
  • Read each AI answer aloud, in order, in a flat voice — no inflection that gives the answer away.
  • Give them 60 seconds to silently pick.
PHASE 2 0:03 — 0:08  ·  THE VOTE
  • Show of hands per answer — tally on the whiteboard.
  • Then click to slide 03 (the reveal). Let it land.
  • Pause on whatever the most popular wrong answer was. That is the lesson.

AI sounds right whether it is or not. Even strong CS students get fooled by confident wrong answers. Today is about closing that gap.

WATCH FOR · DON'T SHAME

Some students will publicly admit they picked wrong. Don't laugh. Don't moralize. Affirm: "Good — that's exactly the instinct we're going to push on for the next 80 minutes."

PAGE 4 · ANCHOR + DEMO

§ 07
02 · The Co-pilot Loop
8 MIN · 00:15 — 00:23 · SLIDE 05
ANCHOR 0:15 — 0:23  ·  HOLD THIS SLIDE

This is the spine of the whole module. Don't rush it. Hold the slide for the full 8 minutes.

DO THIS

  • Walk through FRAME · PROBE · VERIFY · OWN — one minute each.
  • For each step, ask: "What does a 'bad ask' look like at this step?" Take 1 student answer.
  • Then offer the co-pilot version.
  • Last 2 min: explain the loop (OWN → FRAME with a sharper question).

SAY THIS

FRAME: tell AI what it doesn't know. Your class, your goal, your level.

PROBE: the first answer is rarely the best one. Ask follow-ups.

VERIFY: run it. Read it. Don't trust it.

OWN: could you write this from scratch tomorrow? If no — go back to FRAME.

KEY PHRASE TO WRITE ON THE BOARD

"AI is a mirror — vague in, vague out." This sentence will do more lifting than any other in the module.

§ 08
03 · Race the AIs
12 MIN · 00:23 — 00:35 · SLIDE 06 · LIVE DEMO
LIVE 0:23 — 0:35  ·  PROJECTOR ON
  • Open ChatGPT or Claude on the projector. Bigger font.
  • Run prompt V1 (Vague). Show how generic the answer is.
  • Run prompt V2 (with FRAME). Pause: "What's still missing?" Take answers.
  • Let the class propose what V3 should add. Add their suggestions live.
  • Run prompt V3 (full Loop). Read the response together.

IF THE LIVE AI MISBEHAVES

It happens. Have the canned answers from the module page open in a third tab as backup. Don't apologize — the inconsistency is itself a lesson: "see, even the same prompt twice gives you different answers. That's why VERIFY exists."

↓ 0:35 — 0:37  ·  TRANSITION TO LAB · SLIDE 07

In 5 minutes you're going to do this with one of YOUR assignments. Open Canvas now. Pull up something you have due in the next two weeks. Any class.

  • Walk the room while they open Canvas.
  • Anyone who's stuck — sit next to them. Don't make it a thing.

PAGE 5 · LAB + AUDIT + CLOSE

§ 09
04 · Prompt Lab — the deliverable
25 MIN · 00:37 — 01:02 · SLIDE 08 · 35 PTS
QUIET WORK 0:37 — 1:02  ·  WALK THE ROOM

SETUP (FIRST 3 MIN)

  • Read the brief aloud (V1 → V2 → V3 + Credit Ledger).
  • Confirm: "Saves to your browser as you go. You will not lose your work."
  • Show the Credit Ledger explicitly — it's the part students under-do.

WHILE THEY WORK (22 MIN)

  • Walk a slow loop. Don't hover.
  • If a student stares at a blank V1 for 4+ min — sit, ask what class.
  • Backup prompt for stuck students: "describe your weekend in 3 sentences." Works fine.
  • At 0:55 — call the 7-minute warning.

COMMON STUCK POINTS

"I don't have any AI questions." → Then your V1 is "explain X concept from class to a 12-year-old." "My V3 looks just like my V2." → Then you skipped PROBE. What follow-ups would you ask?

§ 10
05 · Pair audit
20 MIN · 01:02 — 01:22 · SLIDE 09 · HANDOUT
PAIRS 1:02 — 1:22  ·  HAND OUT PG 6
  • Hand out the printed Pair Audit sheet (page 6 of this guide).
  • Pair students — ideally with someone from a different major.
  • 3 min each to read partner's V3 silently.
  • 5 min each to score on four axes + write a one-sentence verdict.
  • 4 min discussion: focus on the LOWEST score, not the highest.

THE AXIS THEY AVOID

ETHICAL is the axis students dodge. Push it: "Where would you be embarrassed to show your professor this prompt?" If a pair finishes early, have them rewrite each other's V3 to fix the lowest axis.

§ 11
06 · Closer + take-home + Q&A
8 MIN · 01:22 — 01:30 · SLIDES 10–13
CLOSE 1:22 — 1:30
  • Show slide 10 (take-home brief). Read out: 5 days, 50 pts, 4-sentence Friday reflection.
  • Confirm Markdown template is pinned in Canvas.
  • Slide 11 reframe — read it aloud.
  • Slide 12 Q&A — 3–4 minutes. Stay open.
  • Send them off with slide 13.

When you graduate, the people who got hired will be the ones who can use AI without being used by it. That's a skill. It's the skill we just practiced.

COMMON Q&A — BE READY

QuestionHonest answer
"Will my professors let me use AI?"Varies. Check each syllabus. If unclear — ask the prof, in writing, this week.
"Is using AI cheating?"Depends on what AI did vs. what you did. The Credit Ledger is how you find out.
"What if I just use AI for everything?"Try it. The journal will tell you. Then we talk next class.
