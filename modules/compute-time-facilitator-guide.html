PAGE 2 · WHY & WHEN

§ 01Why this module exists

First-year students don't run out of time. They run into it — usually around week 6 — and discover the term has been sprinting at them while they walked. The standard advice ("use a planner") fails because planners assume the student knows where their hours actually go. They don't. Most are off by 15–25 hours per week on the question "how do you spend your 168?"

This module replaces vibes with arithmetic. We treat time the way a CS class treats memory: a fixed budget, allocated on purpose, profiled when things slow down. Students see their real numbers, see the term's collision points coming, and leave with a triage method they can run in 90 seconds the next time they're overwhelmed.

THE ANCHOR CONCEPT You can't make more time. You can see it and spend it. Audit what you have. Map what's coming. Triage what matters. Re-budget tomorrow. Repeat weekly. Compute Time is a verb.

§ 02Outcomes

By the end of class, students will be able to:

  • State their actual weekly hour budget and call out the 2–3 categories where it leaks.
  • Identify the 3 worst spike weeks of the term before they hit them.
  • Triage any task list using urgency × importance and explain why each task is in its quadrant.
  • Build a single-page ABC-ranked list for tomorrow with realistic time estimates.
  • Run a 15-minute Sunday Re-Budget ritual — independently — every week of the term.

PAGE 3 · PREP & OPENING

§ 04Pre-class checklist (24 hrs out)

  • Open the module page on the projector — confirm internet, screen, audio.
  • Tell students in advance to bring all their syllabi for this term.
  • Have your own filled 168 grid ready to share — model it.
  • Print 1× Sunday Ritual handout per student (page 6 of this guide).
  • Confirm Canvas has the take-home Start Log template pinned.
  • Whiteboard markers for the spike chart group reveal.
  • Stopwatch / timer visible to students during the AUDIT.
  • Glance at last week's reflections — flag anyone who said "I'm drowning."
§ 05
00 · Sticker shock
5 MIN · 00:00 — 00:05 · SLIDES 02–03
OPENER 0:00 — 0:05  ·  LIVE GUESS

Before you do anything else, guess: how many hours are in a week? Don't do the math — guess. Then guess how many of those hours you spend on screens you didn't choose.

  • Display the live counter. Have everyone shout the first number — most will overshoot or undershoot.
  • Reveal: 168. Let the number sit. Don't editorialize.
  • Show the sleep/class/work subtraction. Watch the room do the math.
  • Land on the takeaway: "Whatever's left is the only time you control. Today we find out what 'left' actually is."

WATCH FOR · DON'T LECTURE

If a student says "I have negative hours" — good. That's the truest answer in the room. Affirm it: "Then we have something concrete to work with." Don't fix it yet.

§ 06
01 · Cold open
5 MIN · 00:05 — 00:10 · SLIDE 04
FULL 5 MIN 0:05 — 0:10
  • Click through Jordan, Maya, Ana. Read each card aloud — including the diagnostic line.
  • Pause after each: "Show of hands — who's been Jordan / Maya / Ana this term?"
  • End on the handoff: same surface (overwhelmed), different leak.

Three students, three different broken time budgets. If you tell all three of them to "use a planner," you fix one and waste the other two. The fix has to match the leak.

PAGE 4 · AUDIT & MAP

§ 08
03 · AUDIT — 168-hour grid
15 MIN · 00:18 — 00:33 · SLIDE 06 · 30 PTS
QUIET WORK 0:18 — 0:33  ·  ALL FILLING THE GRID

SETUP (FIRST 3 MIN)

  • Read the brief: paint each cell with a category — sleep, class, study, work, social, screens, transit, other.
  • Show your filled grid first. Be honest about your screen-time block.
  • Confirm: "Saves to your browser as you paint. You won't lose this."

WHILE THEY WORK (12 MIN)

  • Walk a slow loop. Stay quiet. Most students will find this surprisingly hard.
  • If a student stares at "screens" — whisper: "Phone screen-time settings have your number. Pull it up."
  • If a student finishes early, prompt: "Now color the cells you'd want to take back."
  • At 0:31 — call the 2-minute warning.

WHAT THE TOTALS USUALLY SHOW

Most first-years sleep less than they think (avg 50/wk vs claimed 56), study less than they think (avg 12/wk vs claimed 20), and screen more than they think (avg 25/wk vs claimed 12). The arithmetic is the lesson — don't lecture, just point at the bars.

04 · MAP — 15-week spike chart
10 MIN · 00:33 — 00:43 · SLIDE 07
GROUP REVEAL 0:33 — 0:43

The example chart students see — typical first-year load with two fall-term peaks:

123456789101112131415
  • Read the brief: each student opens every syllabus, marks every deliverable on the strip.
  • 10-min sprint. Walking quietly is fine.
  • At 0:42 — show your own spike chart on screen. The spikes are not surprises if you saw them coming.

Look at your chart. The bad weeks are not "out there somewhere." They have dates. The strategy isn't to work harder during them — it's to work earlier before them.

IF A STUDENT HAS NO SPIKES YET

Some students haven't read past week 4 of any syllabus. Don't shame — say: "This exercise just bought you future-you's worst week back. You're welcome." Then they finish the chart in office hours.

↓ 0:43 — 0:45  ·  TRANSITION TO TRIAGE · SLIDE 08

Now we have the data. We know where your hours go and we know where the term gets hard. Next 20 minutes: the method you'll use the next time it's 11pm and you have six things on your list.

  • Tell students to surface this week's actual to-do list — Canvas, notes app, whatever they already have.
  • Walk the room. Anyone with a blank list? Whisper: "List 5 things due in the next 7 days. Anything counts. Go."

PAGE 5 · TRIAGE + RE-BUDGET + CLOSE

§ 10
05 · TRIAGE — Eisenhower matrix
20 MIN · 00:45 — 01:05 · SLIDE 08 · 25 PTS
DRAG-DROP 0:45 — 1:05  ·  INTERACTIVE
  • Read the brief: every task to one of four quadrants. Urgent×Important = DO. Important not Urgent = SCHEDULE. Urgent not Important = DELEGATE/SHRINK. Neither = DELETE.
  • 3 minutes silent: drag every task from this week's list into a quadrant.
  • 10 minutes: the hard part — find at least one task to put in DELETE. Most students refuse.
  • Pair-share for 5 min: show your matrix to a neighbor. Ask: "Would you actually delete that? Why not?"

If your DELETE quadrant is empty, you are lying to yourself about what's important. There is always something. The hardest skill in this module is permission to not do the thing.

THE QUADRANT STUDENTS DODGE

SCHEDULE (important, not urgent) is empty for most first-years — that's where studying lives. Studying never feels urgent until the night before. If a student's SCHEDULE quadrant is empty, that's the problem; their term is run entirely on emergencies.

§ 11
06 · RE-BUDGET — tomorrow's ABC list
12 MIN · 01:05 — 01:17 · SLIDE 09 · 25 PTS
QUIET WORK 1:05 — 1:17
  • Read aloud: A = today, non-negotiable (max 3). B = today if A is done. C = nice if there's air.
  • Each task gets a realistic time estimate. Realistic. Not aspirational.
  • Tally the A column. If A > 4 hours, cut. If A < 1 hour, tomorrow is wasted — add.
  • Last 3 min: 2–3 students read their list aloud. Class votes: realistic / not. Recalibrate.

THE GIVEAWAY TELL

Watch for students who write 8+ A's. That's not a list, it's a wish. Privately: "What gets cut to B if you have a normal 4 hours of focused time?" The answer is the actual list.

§ 12
07 · Take-home + closer
13 MIN · 01:17 — 01:30 · SLIDES 10–12
CLOSE 1:17 — 1:30
  • Hand out the Sunday Ritual sheet (page 6). Walk through it.
  • Show the Time Log template — 7 days, one row per day, 3 fields: A's planned / A's done / what leaked.
  • Confirm: due Sunday 8pm. Late = full credit if turned in by Monday class.
  • Closer slide. Read it aloud. Don't soften it.
  • Q&A — 3–5 minutes. Stay open.

You can't make more time. Nobody in this room ever will. But every Sunday from now on, you can spend the next 168 on purpose instead of by accident. That's the whole skill. The grid, the chart, the matrix, the list — those are the four moves. Run them weekly. Email me when something breaks.

COMMON Q&A — BE READY

QuestionHonest answer
"What if my week never goes to plan?"Good. The plan was never the point — the data is. Compare A's planned vs A's done. The gap is your signal.
"My Sunday is busy. Can I do this Saturday?"Yes. Pick one day, the same day every week, before tomorrow's first deliverable. Consistency > calendar.
"My screen-time number is embarrassing."So is mine. The number is data, not character. Re-budget two of those hours, see what happens.
"What if I have a real job and no choice?"The audit still works — you have fewer cells, that's all. The Eisenhower matrix matters more, not less.
