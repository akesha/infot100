PAGE 2 · WHY & WHEN

§ 01Why this module exists

The default Week 1 of an intro college course is a syllabus walk and a lecture about expectations. Students leave with anxiety, not capability. In a Luddy maker-rich environment, that's a particular waste — the room itself is the curriculum, and most students never figure that out until Week 8.

Spawn Point inverts this. Class meets in the Creator Commons. The first thing students do is post something they made before age 12 — proof that they're already makers. Then they meet 8 capabilities (not 8 machines), pick which ones their final project will need, and physically build a cardboard prototype before they leave. By the 90-minute mark, every student has used their hands, has an opinion about what they'll ship, and has booked time with one piece of equipment for the week.

THE ANCHOR CONCEPT Don't learn the tools. Learn the verbs. Cut, print, stitch, press, cast, protect, capture, design. The verb is portable; the specific machine model isn't. A student who knows the 8 verbs can walk into any maker-space on the planet and find the equivalent tool.

§ 02Outcomes

By the end of class, students will be able to:

  • Name the 8 capabilities of the Creator Commons in their own words.
  • Match at least three capabilities to a specific final-project idea of their own.
  • Build a working cardboard prototype that solves a real problem they had this week.
  • Reflect on what worked, what broke, and what they'd change with 30 more minutes.
  • Commit to one piece of equipment they'll have hands on by the end of the week.

PAGE 3 · PREP & OPENING

§ 04Pre-class checklist (24 hrs out)

  • Confirm Creator Commons reservation. Class meets in the room, not a lecture hall.
  • Pre-cut cardboard stock — ~3 medium boxes' worth, broken flat. Stack on materials table.
  • Materials: scissors (10+), masking tape (4 rolls), hot-glue guns (3, plus glue sticks), Sharpies, rulers.
  • Walk the floor with the Commons staff lead. Confirm which equipment students may approach today (look-only OK).
  • Open the module page on the projector. Test the timer in §4.
  • Print 1× Capability Reference card per student (page 6 of this guide).
  • Have ONE personal "thing I made before 12" ready to share — yours, embarrassing, fine.
  • Glance at the equipment-booking page (iub.libcal.com/equipment?lid=8812) — confirm the URL still works.
§ 05
00 · Roll call — Maker Wall
12 MIN · 00:00 — 00:12 · SLIDES 02–03
PHASE 1 0:00 — 0:04  ·  SETUP + YOU GO FIRST

You're in this room because someone decided you're a maker. You might be skeptical of that. Fair. So before we do anything else — let's prove it. Think back to before you turned 12. What did you make? Cardboard fort, friendship bracelet, slime, a Lego thing you ignored the instructions on. Anything physical. Type it in. Anonymous. The wall fills up.

  • Project the Maker Wall. Type your answer first — model the embarrassment level.
  • Wait 2 minutes. Don't fill silence. Notes will start appearing.
  • If the wall stalls under 8 entries, click + examples to seed it.
PHASE 2 0:04 — 0:12  ·  READ + LAND
  • Read 6–8 entries aloud. Don't editorialize. Let the room laugh.
  • Ask: "Anyone surprised what showed up here?" Take 1–2 reactions.
  • Land on the green callout: every entry on the wall = proof of making.

You're not learning to make. You already make. This semester just sharpens the toolkit. The cardboard you'll touch in 45 minutes is the same impulse — just with better glue.

WATCH FOR · THE STUDENT WHO CAN'T THINK OF ANYTHING

Almost always means they're filtering for "good enough to share." Whisper: "Worst thing you ever made counts. Slime that ruined the carpet counts." Reframe lowers the bar.

ANCHOR 0:18 — 0:26  ·  THE SPINE OF THE MODULE

Don't list all 8 in a row — that becomes a tour. Walk the room as you go. Point at each tool while you say its verb.

01 · CUT
CUT

02 · 3DP
PRINT

03 · SEW
STITCH

04 · PRSS
PRESS
Heat-bond vinyl/sublimation onto fabric or hard surfaces.
05 · RES
CAST
High-detail resin parts. Smaller, smoother, harder.
06 · LAM
PROTECT
Laminator. Demo prototypes that survive a backpack.
07 · CAM
CAPTURE
~40 cameras. Photo, video, 3D-scan equipment.
08 · SW
SOFTWARE
Tinkercad, Silhouette Studio, Adobe (free for IU).

Pick the verb first, then the tool follows. If you walk in saying "I want to use the 3D printer," you'll force a project around the printer. If you walk in saying "I want to PRINT something" — well, now you have options. Including "this isn't a printing problem; it's a stitching one."

WRITE ON THE BOARD

"Verbs over tools." Repeat this phrase every time a student names a machine in the next 90 minutes.

§ 08
03 · Equipment scavenger — Deliverable #1
20 MIN · 00:26 — 00:46 · SLIDE 06 · 30 PTS
QUIET WORK 0:26 — 0:46  ·  WALK THE ROOM

SETUP (FIRST 3 MIN)

  • Have everyone open the module page on their laptop. Confirm the Scavenger section auto-saves.
  • Read the brief: "For each tool, name a real object you'd make for your final project."
  • Stress: specific beats clever. "A phone stand" beats "something cool."

WHILE THEY WORK (17 MIN)

  • Walk a slow loop. Point at each piece of physical equipment as students reach it.
  • Stuck student? Ask: "What thing has annoyed you in the last 7 days?" That's the seed.
  • Encourage students to walk over and touch the equipment as they fill it in.
  • At 0:42 — 4-min warning, encourage rating each tool (must / maybe / no).

COMMON STUCK POINT · "I CAN'T THINK OF ANYTHING FOR THIS TOOL"

That's a valid answer — rate it "no." The point is not to use everything; it's to know what's possible. A student with 3 must-haves and 5 nos has done excellent thinking.

PAGE 5 · BUILD + SEED + CLOSE

§ 09
04 · Cardboard prototype sprint — Deliverable #2
30 MIN · 00:46 — 01:16 · SLIDE 07 · 40 PTS
HANDS ON 0:46 — 1:16  ·  THIS IS THE WHOLE MODULE

Four phases: Pick (3) → Sketch (5) → Build (18) → Photo + Reflect (4). The in-page timer enforces this. Don't let the build phase eat the photo phase — the reflection IS the deliverable.

PHASE 1 · PICK (3 min)

  • Read the prompt aloud. Stress: tiny problems are better than big ones.
  • If 30%+ of the room is staring blank at 1:30 in, click "Stuck? Pick one of these" and read 3 seed problems aloud.

PHASE 2 · SKETCH (5 min)

  • Paper is fine. Tinkercad is fine. Whatever's faster.
  • Don't let students spend 4 of these 5 minutes "perfecting" the sketch.

PHASE 3 · BUILD (18 min)

  • Get them up. "Stand up, walk to the materials table, grab cardboard."
  • Move physically — body language tells students it's OK to leave the chair.
  • If a student stalls 5 min in: "What's the worst possible version of this? Make that. We'll iterate."
  • At 1:11 — 5-min warning. Stop building, take photos.

PHASE 4 · PHOTO + REFLECT (4 min)

  • Phone photos to themselves. The reflection (worked / broke / next) is the graded part.
  • Ask 1–2 students to hold up their thing as they read their reflection.

Senior designers prototype in cardboard their entire careers. Not because they can't afford 3D printers — because cardboard is the fastest way to find out if your idea is dumb. Better to find out in 18 minutes than after a 6-hour Bambu print.

SAFETY · CARDBOARD ONLY TODAY

Today is cardboard, scissors, tape, hot glue. Students should NOT touch the powered cutters, presses, or printers in this session — even if their idea "needs" the ChompSaw. The point is fast iteration, not the final material. Wait until Week 2 (powered-tool orientation).

§ 10
05 · Project seed — Deliverable #3
10 MIN · 01:16 — 01:26 · SLIDE 08 · 30 PTS
QUIET WORK 1:16 — 1:26
  • Section auto-pulls students' "must-have" capabilities from §3 — point that out.
  • Read the 4 prompts: WHO / WHAT problem / WHICH 2–3 capabilities / WHAT'S THE ROUGH SHAPE.
  • Stress: this is a seed, not an answer. They'll iterate it for 14 weeks.
  • Last 2 min: ask 2–3 students to read their seed aloud. Celebrate specificity.

If you can't answer 'who is it for' with a real human in mind, your project will drift. 'College students' isn't a who. 'My roommate Devon' is.

§ 11
06 · Take-home + closer
4 MIN · 01:26 — 01:30 · SLIDE 09
CLOSE 1:26 — 1:30
  • Show the 3 take-homes: install software · book one piece of equipment · post photo + reflection.
  • The booking is the heart of it — physical commitment beats abstract intention.
  • Read the closer aloud. Don't soften it.

Welcome to Luddy. The toolbox is open. You know what the verbs are. By Friday you'll have used at least one. By Week 14, you'll have shipped something nobody else has ever made.

COMMON Q&A — BE READY

QuestionHonest answer
"What if I'm not a CS major?"Even better. The toolbox doesn't care. Half of last year's standout final projects came from students who picked up CS as a minor.
"Do I have to use 3D printing?"No. Jordan's board game didn't. Knowing what NOT to use is the same skill.
"My idea is dumb."Right now, every idea is. That's why it's a seed. By Week 6 it'll be your fifth one and you'll know which one's real.
"What if I don't have a problem to prototype today?"You do. Look around your dorm. Something annoyed you yesterday. Pick that.
