Which roles
does each assignment ask for?

For every Unit 1 assignment, we name the learner role(s) it actually invokes. Following the protocol: if a role doesn't honestly match — we don't add one. Empty cells are findings, not failures.

SERAFINI, F. (2012). READING WORKSHOP 2.0. THE READING TEACHER, 66(4).

The four roles, in plain language

① NAVIGATOR
Navigator
"How do I crack the disciplinary code?"
Pattern recognition. Decoding new conventions, jargon, and graphic / textual signals specific to the domain. Using prior knowledge to predict unknown terms.
② INTERPRETER
Interpreter
"What does this mean to me?"
Bringing personal experience and prior learning to bear. Connecting new content to one's own life, interests, and worldview. Drawing inferences across contexts.
③ DESIGNER
Designer
"What do I do with this information?"
Creating a product that follows the form and conventions of the domain — diagrams, prototypes, presentations, journals — and that can be assessed against criteria.
④ INTERROGATOR
Interrogator
"What does this content allow me to see?"
Examining the values, biases, and intentions behind information. Recognizing whose interests are served, what's left unsaid, and how framing shapes meaning.

WHAT THE EMPTY CELLS TELL US Interrogator is the under-served role across the unit — and that's an honest finding. Unit 1 is about installing the loop, not yet critiquing it. The recommended fix lives in Unit 2 (a "Reading the Code" lesson where students interrogate AI outputs, README claims, and tutorial framings). We do not retrofit Interrogator into making activities just to fill the column.

1 SPAWN
POINT 		Capability Map For each of 8 Creator-Commons verbs, write a real idea + must/maybe/no. 30 ● Eight verbs are the disciplinary metalanguage. Students are decoding what CUT vs. PRINT vs. CAST means as a capability — pattern-recognizing the makerspace. ● Each verb gets translated through the student's own intended project. Pure interpreter move: "what does PRESS mean to my project?" ◐ A grid of must/maybe/no ratings is a small designed artifact, but the rubric grades the thinking, not the form. ○ No critique of which capabilities the lab chose to stock or omit. (Open question for Unit 2.)
Cardboard Prototype Pick a real annoyance from this week. Sketch → build → photograph → write worked / broke / next. 40 ◐ Some pattern-spotting in materials (which tape holds, which fold creases) but the conventions are mostly intuitive. ● Required to surface a problem from their week, not a stock prompt. "What does this annoy in my life?" is the entire entry point. ● Quintessential designer assignment — a physical artifact assessed against a rubric, plus a multimodal reflection (photo + 3-line text). Right out of Serafini's designer skill list. ○ No critique of consumer-product framing or whose problems get prototyped first.
Project Seed WHO + problem + object + 2–3 verbs + smallest v0.1 by Week 4. 30 ◐ Uses the verb metalanguage from the Capability Map. Adds the convention of "WHO is one specific person." ● Asks students to name a real human (often someone they know) and a real pain. Their interpretation of "who is this for" shapes everything downstream. ● A structured five-field design brief — explicit form and conventions of the domain. Becomes the README in Week 3. ○ No examination of whose seeds get encouraged or how "specificity" framing favors certain backgrounds.
2 RECON 168-hr Time Audit Fill the 168-cell weekly grid with what actually happened (not aspirational). 20 ● Decoding the grid as a representational form. Recognizing fixed vs. discretionary blocks is a domain-specific reading move. ● Audit is entirely first-person. "What does my week mean for the 6 hrs/wk this project needs?" is the whole point. ◐ The completed grid is a designed artifact, but the form is given — students fill, not design. ○ No critique of productivity framing or which time uses get coded as "discretionary." Honest gap.
Loadout Check Install + verify dev environment. Screenshot a first-command success. 20 ● Pure decoding. Terminal output, error messages, path conventions, file extensions — every artifact in this assignment is "learn the convention." ○ Setup is identical for everyone; no personal interpretation surface. Honest gap — and that's fine for a baseline check. ◐ Screenshot is a tiny designed artifact, but composition is incidental. ○ No examination of why this stack vs. another. (Reasonable to defer to Week 7.)
Cohort Intro Post One intro in the channel: who you are, what you made before age 12, your seed. 10 ◐ Learning channel etiquette and threading conventions counts as decoding the disciplinary norms of CS community spaces. ● Entirely first-person. Reading peers' intros invites comparison: "where do my interests overlap with theirs?" ○ A 200-word post isn't a designed artifact in the rubric sense. ○ No critique of who feels welcome in CS spaces, though peer reading sets the conditions for it later.
3 SETUP
QUEST 		Project Repo + README Initialize the repo. README names WHO, problem, capabilities — lifted from the Project Seed. 40 ● Heaviest decoding load in the unit. Repo structure, README conventions, markdown syntax, branch naming — all domain conventions, all new. ◐ Translating personal seed text into README form requires real interpretation — but the heavy lifting was Week 1. ● README is the canonical multimodal designer artifact in software. Graded against form-and-convention criteria a stranger could parse. ○ No examination of README rhetoric or whose voice gets coded as "professional." Defer to Unit 2's "Reading the Code" lesson.
First Commit (cardboard photos) Add Week-1 prototype photos to the repo. One green-checkmark commit on the right branch. 10 ● Git verbs (add / commit / push) are pure pattern + convention work. Branch names, commit message format, the green-checkmark visual cue — all decoding. ○ A commit is mechanical; no personal interpretation surface here. Honest absence. ◐ The commit message itself is a tiny designed artifact ("fix: …" / "feat: …"), but minor weight. ○ Not yet — version-control politics is an Interrogator topic for a later unit.
4 SEED
v0.1 		v0.1 Build + Demo Build a v0.1 using a "must" verb. Hand it to a classmate. 30-second demo, unrehearsed. 50 ◐ Domain conventions for what "demo-able" looks like — but most decoding moved to Weeks 1–3. ● Watching a peer touch your object forces you to interpret their reaction — and re-interpret your own seed. ● The capstone designer move of Unit 1: a real product, real form, against criteria. Multimodal: object + spoken demo. ◐ First time Interrogator surfaces in the unit: handing your work to a user invites questioning of assumptions you didn't know you'd made.
Peer Critique Log Receive feedback from one classmate. Don't defend. Log: worked / broke / next. 20 ○ Free-form notes; no new conventions to crack. ● Receiving critique is interpretation work — translating someone else's reaction into actionable meaning for your project. ◐ A structured 3-field log is a small designed artifact — same form as the Cardboard reflection, building consistency. ● Strongest Interrogator moment in Unit 1. Asks: whose feedback do I weight? What did the peer not see? What did I hide in the demo?
Revised Seed (v0.2) Rewrite the Project Seed in light of v0.1 + critique. Note one cut, one keep, one change. 10 ○ Same form as v0.1 seed; conventions already decoded. ● Revision is interpretation made visible — mapping new experience onto the original frame. ● Producing a versioned brief — a domain-canonical designer artifact (v0.1 → v0.2 is itself a CS convention). ◐ "What did I previously assume that turned out to be wrong?" — a beginner Interrogator move on one's own past work.

READING THE LEGEND

● primary — assignment can't be done without this role ◐ supporting — present but not central ○ not currently supported — honest absence

UNIT 1 IS DESIGNER-HEAVY

7 of 11 assignments are primary Designer moments. This is intentional — Unit 1 installs the make-notice-iterate loop. Interpreter is the close second (8 of 11), driven by the seed-to-v0.1 personal-project spine.

WHERE TO STRENGTHEN

Interrogator is under-served (1 primary, 3 supporting, 7 absent). The Peer Critique Log opens the door at Week 4. Unit 2's "Reading the Code" lesson should carry the load — not retrofit Unit 1.
