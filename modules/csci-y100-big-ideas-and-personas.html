FIVE BIG IDEAS — DOMAIN-LEVEL ANCHORS

What we want them
to still believe
in five years.

PER THE BIG-IDEAS PROTOCOL · "I WANT MY STUDENTS TO UNDERSTAND THAT…"

Each idea is enduring (not detail), generalizes across >2 examples, and could anchor an entire course on its own. We commit to teaching five — fewer means lower cognitive load and higher retention.

01

Making is iteration, not inspiration.

I WANT STUDENTS TO UNDERSTAND THATany made thing — software, hardware, prose, art — is the residue of many small loops of try → notice → adjust, not the output of a singular flash of insight. The first version is supposed to be wrong.

EXAMPLES THIS GENERALIZES TO Cardboard prototypes · git commits · debugging sessions · README revisions · the writing process · scientific method · design crits.
02

Verbs over tools — capabilities precede equipment.

I WANT STUDENTS TO UNDERSTAND THATtools change every five years; the underlying capability (cut, print, capture, version, query, visualize) is durable. Good practitioners think in verbs first and pick tools second — which lets them adapt as the toolchain shifts.

EXAMPLES THIS GENERALIZES TO Makerspace 8-verb map · choosing a programming language for a task · picking the right ML library · job-skill descriptions · resume framing · LLM tool-use.
03

Time is a system you instrument, not a virtue you possess.

I WANT STUDENTS TO UNDERSTAND THAT"good time management" is not a personality trait. It's an observable system — a budget, a calendar, a tracked backlog — that anyone can build and tune. Procrastination is a debugging problem, not a moral failure.

EXAMPLES THIS GENERALIZES TO 168-hr time audit · sprint planning · CPU scheduling metaphor · study blocks · OS process model · personal kanban · burnout prevention.
04

Knowledge worth keeping must be externalized.

I WANT STUDENTS TO UNDERSTAND THATwhat lives only in your head is fragile. Durable practitioners build a "second brain" — notes, repos, READMEs, version control, peer channels — so that what they learn compounds across semesters instead of evaporating between finals and August.

EXAMPLES THIS GENERALIZES TO Git history as memory · README as user manual · Obsidian vault · lab notebook · code comments · data documentation · scientific reproducibility.
05

Tools — including AI — are collaborators to interrogate, not oracles to obey.

I WANT STUDENTS TO UNDERSTAND THATevery tool has a worldview baked into it: a textbook, a tutorial, a Stack Overflow answer, an LLM completion. Skilled use means knowing the tool's bias, naming what it can't see, and keeping ownership of the judgment call.

EXAMPLES THIS GENERALIZES TO Co-Pilot Camp lesson · README rhetoric · peer critique · evaluating sources · using AI without using yourself up · ethics in CS · tool selection.

How each statement passes the protocol's test

Every Big Idea above (a) completes "I want students to understand that…", (b) names >2 generalizable examples, (c) is an enduring concept rather than a fact a student could simply look up. None of them name a specific tool, week, or assignment — that's by design.

Persona 01. Aspirational.

THE IDEAL STUDENT THE LEARNER WE'RE DESIGNING FOR
MNASP

Maya Nakamura

FIRST-YEAR · UNDECIDED → INFORMATICS · 18 YRS
EDUCATIONAL OVERVIEW Public-school student from Indianapolis. Took AP CSP (3) and ran a 3D-printing club junior year. Rebuilt the family's WiFi setup over the summer "for fun." Has never written production software but has shipped a half-dozen small things her friends actually use.
Maya already identifies as a maker. She's here to translate that hobbyist instinct into a degree path — and to find a cohort that takes her seriously even though she didn't go to a feeder magnet school. She'll over-commit if you let her, and she'll keep showing up even when it's hard. When I'm building something for my dorm or a friend, I lose three hours easy. I want this class to feel like that — not like a syllabus quiz.

CONTENT KNOWLEDGE

  • AP CSP (3) — comfortable with Python basics, lists, conditionals
  • Tinkercad + a desktop 3D printer at home; ran 50+ prints
  • Reads documentation when she has to; not afraid of the terminal
  • Has never used git, branches, or a code review

MOTIVATION FOR TAKING COURSE

  • Wants to confirm CS / Informatics is the right major
  • Wants a v0.1 of something real on her resume by November
  • Hopes to find a cohort and 1–2 collaborators
  • Curious about AI — wants to use it without losing her own thinking

ENGAGEMENT STRATEGIES

  • Will attend every studio + most office hours
  • Posts in the cohort channel within 48 hrs of joining
  • Treats the syllabus as a contract; reads ahead
  • Will iterate on a project past the due date if she's hooked

WHAT THIS PERSONA ASKS OF THE COURSE

RAISE THE CEILINGOptional "stretch" deliverables on every assignment so she's never bored.
NAME THE DEEP STRUCTUREBig Ideas 01 + 02 ("iteration," "verbs over tools") give language to instincts she already has.
PROVIDE A PEER NETWORKThe cohort channel + Week-4 peer critique are her real reason for showing up to a brick-and-mortar class.
DON'T PATRONIZE HER PRIOR WORKThe Maker Wall surfaces her existing work in Week 1 minute 5 — she leaves the first session feeling seen.

DRMED

Devon Reyes

FIRST-YEAR · COMPUTER SCIENCE (DECLARED) · 18 YRS
EDUCATIONAL OVERVIEW Suburban high school, no AP CS offered. Self-taught a little Python from YouTube. Picked CS because the family thinks it's "stable" — Devon thinks it's interesting but isn't sure. Holds a 12 hr/wk on-campus job.

Falls behind not from inability but from never being shown how a college week is supposed to be structured. I think I want to do CS but I don't know if I'm 'a CS person.' Half the people in the lab seem to already know everything.

CONTENT KNOWLEDGE

  • Knows what a variable, loop, and function are; shaky on classes
  • Has used a text editor; has never touched a real terminal
  • Has never used git, READMEs, or code review
  • Confident with ChatGPT — sometimes too confident

MOTIVATION FOR TAKING COURSE

  • Required for the major
  • Quietly wants to belong — to feel like "a CS person"
  • Wants a clear weekly rhythm so they don't fall behind
  • Wants to know if AI will replace the career before they finish it

ENGAGEMENT STRATEGIES

  • Attends most classes; rarely speaks unless asked
  • Submits things on time — but at 11:54 PM
  • Will not attend office hours unsolicited (assumes they're "for dumb questions")
  • Will respond to a direct DM in the cohort channel

WHAT THIS PERSONA ASKS OF THE COURSE

MAKE THE FIRST WEEK A DELIVERABLE, NOT A LECTURESpawn Point's three-artifact day is calibrated for this student — proof of work in 90 min.
EXPLICITLY DEFINE BELONGINGMaker Wall and "you already make things" framing directly counter "I'm not a CS person."
GIVE THEM A VISIBLE WEEKLY RHYTHMPacing guide is for them as much as the instructor — predictability lowers cortisol.
NORMALIZE THE LOADOUTLoadout Check (Week 2) makes the hidden curriculum explicit so first-gen students aren't blocked silently.

Persona 03. The student we're losing.

THE D / F / W STUDENT ~18% OF Y100 · WITHDRAWS BY WK 6
JOD/F/W

Jordan Okeke

FIRST-YEAR · UNDECLARED · 19 YRS · FIRST-GENERATION
EDUCATIONAL OVERVIEW First in family to attend a 4-year university. Graduated from a high school that offered no CS courses. Works 25 hrs/wk at a logistics warehouse off-campus to cover rent. Took Y100 because an advisor said "you can take anything." Sat in the back of the room.
Jordan disappears around Week 4 every semester. Not because they don't care — because by then they've had two weeks of feeling lost, the laptop has a Python install error nobody helped them fix, the cohort channel feels cliquey, and the next assignment is opaque. They withdraw silently, often with no instructor contact. I just stopped opening Canvas. By then I was so far behind I figured it was easier to take the W and try again next year.

CONTENT KNOWLEDGE

  • No prior coding exposure
  • Strong informal making — fixes cars, builds shelving, did stage tech in HS
  • Owns a Chromebook; has never installed dev software
  • Uses ChatGPT to "translate" assignments they can't decode

MOTIVATION FOR TAKING COURSE

  • Curious about CS but unsure if it's "for them"
  • Needs the credit — financial aid requires full-time enrollment
  • Wants to feel like the tuition was worth it
  • Quietly hopes to find one adult on campus who knows their name

ENGAGEMENT CHALLENGES

  • Commute + work make synchronous extras (eve studios) impossible
  • Won't ask for help; assumes asking = proof of not belonging
  • Falls behind once → assumes they can't catch up → withdraws
  • Never sets foot in office hours; doesn't know what they're for

WHAT THIS PERSONA ASKS OF THE COURSE

SURFACE NON-CS MAKING AS LEGITIMATEThe Maker Wall counts car repair and stage tech as proof. Jordan's first-week experience is "oh, I already do this" instead of "I'm behind."
FIX THE LOADOUT IN CLASS, NOT AT HOMELoadout Check moves to a synchronous lab so the broken-Python failure mode never happens in isolation.
PROACTIVE OUTREACH AT WK 3Any student missing two consecutive deliverables gets a personal DM, not an LMS notification. This is the single highest-leverage intervention.
ASYNC PARITY FOR EVERY SYNC EVENTEvery studio has a recorded + chat-replied async path so a 25 hr/wk job isn't a withdrawal sentence.
"OFFICE HOURS DEMYSTIFIED" AS A FORMAL UNITDon't assume students know what office hours are for. Teach the convention explicitly.
TWO SAFE FAILURES BUILT INTO THE GRADEDrop-the-lowest on small deliverables so a single bad week doesn't cascade into a W.

SOURCE · DERIVED FROM Y100 D/F/W RATE · INTERVENTION HERE = LARGEST IMPACT ON COHORT RETENTION AND EQUITY.
